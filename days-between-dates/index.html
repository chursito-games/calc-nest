What this calculator shows

This Days Between Dates Calculator provides several ways to understand the distance between two dates, depending on what kind of result you need.

  • Total days: the raw number of days between the dates.
  • Weekdays only: useful for business days, work timelines, or weekday-only planning.
  • Weekend days: useful when you want to understand non-working days in the range.
  • Total weeks: useful for rough planning in weekly units.
  • Approx. months: a simple estimate based on average month length, not an exact calendar month count.
  • Calendar difference: the span written in years, months, and days.

Inclusive vs exclusive date counting

One of the most common questions with date calculations is whether the end date should be counted.

  • Exclusive count: the end date is not included. This is often used when measuring elapsed time between two dates.
  • Inclusive count: both the start date and end date are included. This can be useful for schedules, event spans, bookings, and some administrative counting methods.

Use the “Include the end date” option when you want the calculator to count the full date range inclusively.

Worked examples

Example 1: project timeline

If a project starts on April 1 and ends on April 30, the raw day difference depends on whether the end date is included.

Exclusive counting measures elapsed time between the dates, while inclusive counting can be more useful when describing the full project window.

Example 2: vacation planning

If a trip starts on July 10 and ends on July 17, some people want the full trip span including both dates, while others only want the elapsed time between departure and return.

The calculator helps with both approaches.

Example 3: payment terms

If an invoice is due 30 days after issue date, total days can help track the exact due window, while weekdays-only results can help with workday planning.

Example 4: school or contract period

A semester, contract, or subscription period can be described in days, weeks, or calendar months depending on what kind of reporting or planning you need.

Why “approximate months” is only an estimate

Calendar months do not all have the same number of days. Some months have 31 days, some 30, and February is shorter. Because of that, converting a date span into months is often only an estimate unless you use a full calendar-based month calculation.

That is why this page shows both Approx. months and a separate Calendar difference result.

What happens if the dates are entered in reverse order?

If the later date is entered first and the earlier date is entered second, the calculator still computes the difference correctly by using the two dates as a range. This makes the tool more forgiving and easier to use.

Frequently asked questions

How do you calculate the number of days between two dates?

The calculator compares the start date and end date and measures the date difference directly. You can also choose whether to include the end date.

What does “include end date” mean?

It means the last day in the range is counted as part of the total. This is useful when you want the full date span rather than only elapsed time.

Why are weekdays fewer than total days?

Weekdays exclude Saturdays and Sundays, so the count is usually lower than the full day count.

What are weekend days?

Weekend days are Saturdays and Sundays within the selected date range.

Why is approximate months different from calendar difference?

Approximate months is a simplified estimate, while calendar difference reflects the date span in years, months, and days based on the actual calendar.

Can I use this calculator for future dates?

Yes. You can compare past dates, present dates, or future dates as long as both dates are valid.

What happens if my start date is after my end date?

The calculator still works and computes the difference between the two dates.

Can I use this Days Between Dates Calculator on mobile?

Yes. The page is designed to work on phones, tablets, and desktop devices.
